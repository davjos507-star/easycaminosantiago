The Camino de Santiago:
A thousand years of faith, culture and human adventure

"The Camino de Santiago is the only European route declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site, in 1985. Every year, more than 300,000 people from around the world walk its paths."

Who was the Apostle James?

James the Greater, son of Zebedee, was one of the first four disciples chosen by Jesus, together with Peter, Andrew and John. A fisherman from the Sea of Galilee, Jesus nicknamed him and his brother "Sons of Thunder" for their passionate and determined character.

He was the first apostle to be martyred: King Herod Agrippa I ordered his beheading around 44 AD. According to tradition, his disciples carried his body by ship to the shores of Galicia, where he was buried in what is now Compostela.

In the 9th century, the hermit Pelayo observed mysterious lights over a field. Upon investigation, a tomb was discovered which Bishop Teodomiro identified as that of the Apostle. King Alfonso II ordered a church to be built over the tomb, the seed of today's Cathedral.

The birth of the pilgrimage

News of the discovery spread across Europe and pilgrims soon began arriving from all corners of Christendom. By the 10th century, pilgrimage to Compostela was firmly established, rivalling journeys to Rome and Jerusalem.

The 11th and 12th centuries were its golden age. Kings such as Sancho III and Alfonso VI promoted the route, building roads, bridges and hostels. The Cluniac religious orders organised the infrastructure that would make the Camino Francés famous.

The Camino went through periods of decline — the Black Death, religious wars — but always revived. In 1884, Pope Leo XIII reaffirmed the authenticity of the relics, and the 20th century brought the greatest revival in modern history.

The Holy Year

Celebrated when 25 July — the Apostle's feast day — falls on a Sunday. Pope Callixtus II established this privilege in 1122. During a Holy Year, the Holy Door of the Cathedral opens and pilgrims can obtain a plenary indulgence.

The Compostela

Official certificate issued by the Cathedral. The pilgrim's name is inscribed in Latin, signed by the Secretary of the Chapter. In 1985, 2,491 were issued; today more than 300,000 are awarded each year. Obtained by walking at least 100 km on foot or 200 km by bicycle.

The Pilgrim's Blessing

A medieval tradition celebrated in Roncesvalles at the start of the Camino Francés and in other churches along the route. The clergyman prays that the pilgrim "undertakes the journey and returns safe and well with God's help". A rite that unites faith, history and community.

The route
The Camino of a lifetime

The Camino Francés is the world's most famous Jacobean route. From Saint-Jean-Pied-de-Port, at the foot of the French Pyrenees, to the Cathedral of Santiago, this 780-kilometre route crosses unique landscapes, historic cities and villages that have remained virtually unchanged for centuries.

Along its 33 stages it passes through gems such as Pamplona, Logroño, Burgos and León. The famous Castilian Meseta offers kilometres of silence and reflection. Easy Camino Santiago organises everything: accommodation night by night, daily luggage transfer, pilgrim's credential and 24h support.

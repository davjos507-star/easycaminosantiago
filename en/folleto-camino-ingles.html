History & tradition

The Camino de Santiago:
A thousand years of faith, culture and human adventure

"The Camino de Santiago is the only European route declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site, in 1985. Every year, more than 300,000 people from around the world walk its paths."

Who was the Apostle James?

James the Greater, son of Zebedee, was one of the first four disciples chosen by Jesus, alongside Peter, Andrew and John. A fisherman on the Sea of Galilee, Jesus nicknamed him and his brother "Sons of Thunder" for their passionate and determined character.

He was the first apostle martyr: King Herod Agrippa I ordered his beheading around 44 AD. According to tradition, his disciples transported his body by sea to the coast of Galicia, where he was buried in what is now Compostela.

In the 9th century, the hermit Pelayo observed mysterious lights over a field. When investigated, a tomb was discovered that Bishop Teodomiro identified as the Apostle's. King Alfonso II ordered a church built over the sepulchre — the seed of today's Cathedral.

The birth of the pilgrimage

News of the discovery spread across Europe and pilgrims began arriving from every corner of Christendom. By the 10th century, the pilgrimage to Compostela was firmly established, rivalling journeys to Rome and Jerusalem.

The 11th and 12th centuries were the golden age. Kings such as Sancho III and Alfonso VI promoted the route, building roads, bridges and hostels. The Cluniac religious orders organised the infrastructure that made the Camino Francés world-famous.

The Camino endured periods of decline — the Black Death, the Wars of Religion — but always revived. In 1884, Pope Leo XIII reaffirmed the authenticity of the relics, and the 20th century brought the greatest revival in modern history.

The Holy Year

Celebrated when 25 July — the feast of the Apostle — falls on a Sunday. Pope Callixtus II instituted this privilege in 1122. In a Holy Year, the Holy Door of the Cathedral opens and pilgrims may obtain a plenary indulgence.

The Compostela

The official certificate issued by the Cathedral. The pilgrim's name is inscribed in Latin and signed by the Cathedral Secretary. In 1985 just 2,491 were awarded; today more than 300,000 are issued per year. Requires at least 100 km on foot or 200 km by bicycle.

The Pilgrim's Blessing

A medieval tradition celebrated at Roncesvalles at the start of the Camino Francés and in churches along other routes. The cleric prays that the pilgrim "may undertake the journey and return home safe and sound with God's help." A rite that unites faith, history and community.

Route description
The Way born by the sea

In the Middle Ages, the fame of the Camino de Santiago spread throughout Europe. Pilgrims from the British Isles and Ireland crossed the ocean to the ports of A Coruña and Ferrol to begin their pilgrimage to Santiago. Today we use the Ferrol departure as the most picturesque of the two options.

The Camino Inglés is the only major pilgrimage route that begins right beside the sea, making the first days a truly unique experience: wild coastal scenery before heading into the rural heart of Galicia. The route then winds through the agricultural lands of the interior, giving you the chance to savour authentic Galicia — its people and its food.

After crossing the high ground at Bruma–Mesón do Vento, the descent towards Santiago is gentle and exhilarating. The magnificent Romanesque Cathedral marks the end of your pilgrimage.

Day 1
Arrival in Ferrol
Arrival day Welcome & check-in
You can fly to Santiago de Compostela or A Coruña from numerous European airports, with several daily buses from both to Ferrol. The city is steeped in history: abundant prehistoric remains, its role as a naval base across several historical conflicts, and an architecture that reflects that depth of time. The centre is a delight to wander. Superb seafood and the warmth of the locals make Ferrol one of Spain's hidden gems.
★
Don't miss: the A Magdalena neighbourhood with its Art Nouveau architecture and the finest seafood from the Ría de Ferrol at Ferrol Market. Your pilgrim credential and scallop shell will be waiting for you at your accommodation.

Frequently asked questions
Everything you need to know
How do I get to Ferrol?
You can fly to Santiago de Compostela or A Coruña. From both airports there are several daily buses to Ferrol: from A Coruña with Arriva, from Santiago with Monbus. Journey time is 1 to 1 hr 20 min. Note: Santiago airport will be closed from 23 April to 27 May 2026 for works.
What level of fitness do I need?
The Camino Inglés is rated easy to moderate. Daily stages are 12 to 21 km. The route is well waymarked and we provide detailed route notes. If you're not at peak fitness, the Camino itself will get you there in the first couple of days.
Can I earn the Compostela?
Yes. The Camino Inglés from Ferrol exceeds the 100 km minimum required by the Cathedral of Santiago. You simply need to stamp your credential at least twice per stage. Your credential and scallop shell will be waiting at your first accommodation.
How far in advance should I book?
We recommend booking at least 6 weeks ahead. In high season (July–August) and around bank holidays, book earlier. In low season we can organise your Camino in under a week. Get in touch with no obligation.
Can I walk with my large rucksack?
Yes, though we don't recommend it. Luggage transfer is included in all our packages so you can walk unencumbered. If you prefer to carry everything, we can remove that service and adjust the price accordingly.
What if I can't complete a stage?
Our team in Lugo is available by phone 24 hours a day to help. Public transport and taxis are available throughout the route. We always find a solution.

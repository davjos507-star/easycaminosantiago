History & tradition

The Camino de Santiago:
A thousand years of faith, culture and human adventure

"The Camino de Santiago is the only European route declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site, in 1985. Every year, more than 300,000 people from around the world walk its paths."

Who was the Apostle James?

James the Greater, son of Zebedee, was one of the first four disciples chosen by Jesus, alongside Peter, Andrew and John. A fisherman on the Sea of Galilee, Jesus nicknamed him and his brother "Sons of Thunder" for their passionate and determined character.

He was the first apostle martyr: King Herod Agrippa I ordered his beheading around 44 AD. According to tradition, his disciples transported his body by sea to the coast of Galicia, where he was buried in what is now Compostela.

In the 9th century, the hermit Pelayo observed mysterious lights over a field. When investigated, a tomb was discovered that Bishop Teodomiro identified as the Apostle's. King Alfonso II ordered a church built over the sepulchre — the seed of today's Cathedral.

The birth of the pilgrimage

News of the discovery spread across Europe and pilgrims began arriving from every corner of Christendom. By the 10th century, the pilgrimage to Compostela was firmly established, rivalling journeys to Rome and Jerusalem.

The 11th and 12th centuries were the golden age. Kings such as Sancho III and Alfonso VI promoted the route, building roads, bridges and hostels. The Cluniac religious orders organised the infrastructure that made the Camino Francés world-famous.

The Camino endured periods of decline — the Black Death, the Wars of Religion — but always revived. In 1884, Pope Leo XIII reaffirmed the authenticity of the relics, and the 20th century brought the greatest revival in modern history.

The Holy Year

Celebrated when 25 July — the feast of the Apostle — falls on a Sunday. Pope Callixtus II instituted this privilege in 1122. In a Holy Year, the Holy Door of the Cathedral opens and pilgrims may obtain a plenary indulgence.

The Compostela

The official certificate issued by the Cathedral. The pilgrim's name is inscribed in Latin and signed by the Cathedral Secretary. In 1985 just 2,491 were awarded; today more than 300,000 are issued per year. Requires at least 100 km on foot or 200 km by bicycle.

The Pilgrim's Blessing

A medieval tradition celebrated at Roncesvalles at the start of the Camino Francés and in churches along other routes. The cleric prays that the pilgrim "may undertake the journey and return home safe and sound with God's help." A rite that unites faith, history and community.

The route
The most authentic Camino challenge

The Primitive Way is pure history. In the year 829, King Alfonso II walked this route from Oviedo to the remains of the Apostle James — he was the first official pilgrim. The path he followed is essentially the same one you can walk today. It is the most physically demanding Camino: it crosses the Cantabrian mountains through passes that exceed 1,000 metres.

The Primitive Way passes through Lugo — our home city — which gives us a unique advantage. We know the Galician section perfectly: its best accommodation, its support points, its hidden gems. If you choose the Primitive Way with Easy Camino Santiago, you'll have the backing of people who know this route from the inside.
